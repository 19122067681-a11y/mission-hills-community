Community Background

Mission Hills Upper East District II is a community where people live close, but may not yet know one another

The community has about 364 households and was completed in 2021, so neighbor relationships are still forming. Interviews suggest that about 50-60 households include older adults, and nearly 70% of them are seasonal residents who came to Hainan from other regions. They bring experience, skills, and life wisdom, while also facing everyday connection challenges around healthcare, shopping, smartphone use, and asking neighbors for help.

What Residents Need

The issue is not a lack of people. It is a lack of connection.

01

Daily services feel inconvenient

Healthcare, groceries, repairs, and companionship are everyday needs, but residents do not always know who can help.

02

Information is hard to access

Online registration, community notices, and local service updates often depend on smartphone skills, leaving some older adults behind.

03

Local social ties are weak

Seasonal older adults lack stable neighbor networks. When something happens, they may not know whom to ask or may feel reluctant to speak up.

Community Asset Map

The community already has many resources. They simply need to be connected.

Seasonal older adults

Retired teachers, doctors, electricians, residents who can sew or cook, and community members who enjoy singing, dancing, or sharing stories.

Younger neighbors

Smartphone coaching, online hospital registration, parcel pickup, walking companions, picture-book exchange, English practice, and homework support.

Shared community spaces

The property office entrance, blank corridor walls, notice boards, resident WeChat group, shared activity corner, and resource-card collection point.

How It Works

A four-part toolkit connects real abilities with real needs

1

Neighbor Resource Cards

Residents write down what they can offer or what help they need, and can choose to be contacted through volunteers.

2

Public Information Board

Sorted cards are posted near the property office, notice board, or corridor areas in large-print formats that are easier to read.

3

Mutual-Aid WeChat Group

The group works as an online supplement for help requests and offers, jointly moderated by volunteers and property staff.

4

Silver-Age Volunteers

Volunteers help older adults fill cards, update the board, support offline matching, and reduce the pressure of asking for help.

Neighbor Resource Cards

Blue and orange cards make both offers and needs visible

The blue card records what residents can offer, while the orange card records what help they need. Volunteers organize the information board each week and connect matching cards into a small mutual-aid map.

Co-creation

The activity is not role-play. It invites real people to build community through real lives.

1 minute

Fill a resource card

Each participant writes down one ability they can offer or one need they may have.

1 minute

Match cards on site

The host reads out possible matches, such as “needs online registration” and “can teach smartphone use.”

30 seconds

Post matches to the map

Successful matches are placed on the visiting map, making mutual aid visible.

Next Steps

Sometimes, connection can begin with something as small as a card

The next phase will continue neighbor visits, recruit silver-age volunteers, launch skill-sharing activities, and evaluate impact through resource-card counts, matching frequency, and community connection surveys.

  1. Run more neighbor visits this month
  2. Build a silver-age volunteer team of 5-8 people
  3. Update the public information board every two weeks
  4. Complete at least 10 mutual-aid matches each month
  5. Collect feedback and celebrate new connections
